[LOCATION PROFILE – DOVECOAST & SURROUNDINGS]

Town Name: Dovecoast, Massachusetts  
Population: ~4,200  
Vibe: Fogbound coastal town with colonial bones and a superstition problem. Tourists love the charm. Locals lock their doors before dusk. The sea speaks softly. The land listens harder.

---

LOCAL LANDMARKS

1. Ex Libris  
A repurposed church now serving as a bookstore/café, owned by Carl Ashcombe. High vaulted ceilings, stained glass, shelves built from pews. Basement access is sealed. The espresso’s strong. The shadows linger.

2. The Alabaster Widow  
An old lighthouse turned bar on the cliffs. Named for a local legend of a shipwreck bride. Excellent whiskey. Frequent power flickers. Patrons leave chairs open at the corner tables — just in case.

3. Wick Hollow Cemetery  
One of the oldest in the state. Some tombstones predate the Revolution. Several are written in now-lost dialects. The ground shifts, slightly, seasonally. No one knows why.

4. Graven Hill Hospital (Decommissioned)  
Once a TB sanatorium, now a rotting husk. Local teens sneak in for dares. Occasionally, someone finds fresh boot prints in the dust. Locals swear its morgue tunnels connect to the town’s old root cellars.

5. The Tide & Bone  
Curiosities, antiques, and probably-cursed trinkets. Run by eccentric widow Maura Bell. She claims nothing in her shop is dangerous unless *you already are.*

6. The Salt Lantern Inn  
Coastal inn near the harbor. Quiet, candlelit, and surprisingly warm despite its creaking bones. Offers “spiritual room cleanings” by request — no judgment, no refunds.

7. Dovecoast Archives & Historical Society  
Vaulted ceilings, dusty shelves, and a few locked rooms no one remembers opening. Archivists insist there’s no “third sublevel,” but one researcher went looking. They’re still sorting his notes.

8. Dovecoast Opera House  
Grand, half-renovated, only active three times a year. No one knows who owns the deed. The costume room is sealed, and no one can find the key — not even the current director.

---

SURROUNDING INSTITUTIONS

Hawthorn College  
Location: North of Dovecoast, nestled in Bellmare Woods.  
Founded: 1851  
Specialties: Folklore studies, occult anthropology, maritime history, rare manuscript curation.  
Reputation: Small but elite. Multiple professors have disappeared over the years. Students experience vivid dreams.  
Notable Site: Gideon Hall — home to the folklore archives, ritual artifacts, and a locked lower level that doesn’t appear on official maps.

The Brookes Institute for Parahistory  
A shadowy research foundation with ties to Hawthorn College. Allegedly catalogs “anomalous cultural phenomena.” Officially closed. Lights often stay on past midnight.

---

CHURCHES & SPIRITUAL SITES

Saint Ligeia’s Chapel (Abandoned)  
Built 1794. Bell tower collapsed decades ago. The foundation still holds, barely. Locals claim to hear bells on foggy mornings. There’s a cracked baptismal font beneath the altar.

All Souls Unitarian  
Still active. Accepting. Hosts full moon gatherings and grief circles. Reverend Marnie Griggs, known for both her warmth and her quiet protective rituals, rarely preaches but always listens.

---

NEIGHBORING TOWNS

Ashridge  
15 miles inland. Once an industrial town, now home to music venues and haunted creatives. Home to an annual underground art show called “The Last Light.” Features a Prohibition-era tunnel system that no one has fully mapped.

Briar Glen  
10 miles west. Tiny, pine-choked village. Famous for its handmade crafts and its ominous silence. The woods surrounding it — The Glen — are darker than they should be. Even at noon.

Port Hesper  
30 miles up the coast. Larger harbor town with active shipping and fishing docks. Bustling, but uneasy. The Lantern Pier is infamous — dozens of ships disappeared during the Great Storm of 1898. Some say ghost ships still try to return.

---

NOTABLE NATURAL AREAS

Bellmare Woods  
Dense, wind-twisted forest surrounding Hawthorn College. Covered in moss, old boundary stones, and whisper trails. Some paths loop when they shouldn’t. The birds stop singing in certain clearings.

The Glen (Briar Glen)  
Heavy pine, no underbrush, and dead silence. Said to contain an old stone circle that predates recorded history. Most locals won’t talk about it. Some pretend it isn’t real.

Cradle Bay  
Just east of Dovecoast. A horseshoe-shaped inlet with mirror-still waters and stories of unmarked boats arriving at dawn. Seals gather there in odd numbers. Divers report inexplicable riptides — even during calm tides.

---

Atmosphere Summary:  
Dovecoast is where New England’s shadow hides its face in old lace curtains and briny fog. Every street is a page. Every whisper is a footnote. And something waits in the walls.
